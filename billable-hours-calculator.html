How to Calculate Billable Hours

Calculating billable hours accurately is the foundation of professional invoicing. The core formula is simple: Total Billable Amount = Hourly Rate × Hours Worked. If your rate is $150/hour and you spent 3 hours and 45 minutes on a project, convert the minutes to a decimal (45 ÷ 60 = 0.75), giving you 3.75 hours. Your invoice amount is $150 × 3.75 = $562.50.

For more precise tracking, record your time in increments — many professionals round to the nearest 6 minutes (0.1 hours) or 15 minutes (0.25 hours). The key is consistency: pick an increment and stick with it for all clients so your invoices are defensible.

Don't forget to separate billable from non-billable time. Client calls, project execution, and deliverable revisions are typically billable. Admin tasks, internal meetings, and prospecting are usually not. Keeping a clear split helps you see your true utilization rate — the percentage of working hours you can actually invoice. Most consultants and freelancers aim for a 65–80% utilization rate. If you're consistently below 60%, it's time to either raise your rate or cut down on overhead.

What Is a Fair Billable Rate?

A fair billable rate covers not just your time but all the costs that employment normally absorbs: employer-side taxes, health insurance, retirement contributions, software and equipment, professional development, and the non-billable time you spend on admin and sales. A common freelancer formula is to take the equivalent full-time salary for your role, divide by 2,080 hours (a standard work year), and then multiply by 2 to 3 to arrive at a market-rate billable figure.

For example, a designer whose equivalent salary is $80,000/year earns roughly $38.50/hour as an employee. Multiplied by 2.5, a fair billable rate is approximately $96–$100/hour. Adjust upward for specialized skills, tight deadlines, or a limited client pool. Rates for lawyers, engineers, and senior consultants routinely exceed $200–$400/hour because demand outpaces supply and the downside risk of errors is high.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I calculate billable hours? +
Multiply your hourly rate by the hours worked. For example, $150/hour × 3.5 hours = $525. Track time in decimal format — 30 minutes is 0.5 hours, 15 minutes is 0.25 hours. Enter your rate and hours in the Billable Hours tab above and the calculator does the math instantly, including your daily rate, weekly rate, and a live per-second earnings ticker.
What is a standard billable rate? +
Billable rates vary widely by profession and experience. Lawyers typically charge $200–$600/hour, consultants $100–$400/hour, freelance developers and designers $75–$250/hour, and accountants $100–$300/hour. For freelancers, a useful rule of thumb is to charge 2–3× your equivalent full-time hourly wage to account for taxes, benefits, non-billable overhead, and the absence of employer contributions.
How much does an average meeting cost? +
A one-hour meeting with 5 attendees earning $75/hour costs $375 in combined salary alone — not counting lost productivity or follow-up time. A 90-minute executive meeting with 10 people at $150/hour average costs $2,250. Use the Meeting Cost Mode above to calculate the real cost of any meeting and share the ticking counter with your team as a reminder that meetings have a price tag.
What is the difference between billable and non-billable hours? +
Billable hours are time you can charge a client for — project work, client calls, revisions, and deliverables. Non-billable hours are overhead you absorb: admin tasks, internal meetings, sales calls, and training. Most professionals bill 60–80% of their working hours. The gap between billable and non-billable is your overhead rate. Keeping this low by batching admin and reducing unnecessary meetings directly increases your effective hourly earnings.
