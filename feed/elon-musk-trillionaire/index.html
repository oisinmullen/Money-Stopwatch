Elon Musk Is Now a Trillionaire — Here's How Much He Makes Every Second

Key Facts

  • Date became trillionaire: June 12, 2026
  • Trigger: SpaceX IPO on Nasdaq (ticker: SPCX)
  • IPO price: $135 per share
  • SpaceX valuation: $1.77 trillion
  • Musk net worth at IPO: $1.005 trillion
  • Current estimated net worth: $1.23 trillion

On June 12, 2026, Elon Musk crossed a threshold no human being had ever reached. When SpaceX listed on the Nasdaq under the ticker SPCX, pricing its IPO at $135 a share and valuing the company at approximately $1.77 trillion, Musk's net worth surpassed $1 trillion. He is the world's first trillionaire in recorded history.

The SpaceX IPO raised a record $75 billion — the largest public offering in history, surpassing the $29 billion IPO of Saudi Aramco in 2019. Musk owns 4.8 billion shares of SpaceX, approximately 42% of the company, plus 350 million stock options. At the IPO price his stake was worth $648 billion. Combined with his Tesla holdings, his net worth crossed $1 trillion on the day of the listing. As of June 18, 2026, estimates put his net worth at approximately $1.23 trillion.

How Much Does Elon Musk Make Per Second?

Based on his estimated net worth of $1.23 trillion, Musk's wealth grows by approximately $2,500 every second. That is around $150,000 per minute, $9 million per hour, and roughly $216 million per day. The average American worker earns around $60,000 per year. Musk accumulates that same amount in under 30 seconds.

Why a Trillion Is Genuinely Incomprehensible

Most people struggle to grasp the difference between a million, a billion, and a trillion. Here is the clearest way to think about it — using seconds.

If you started counting from the moment you were born, one second at a time, reaching one million seconds would take just over 11 days. A long holiday. Reaching one billion seconds would take 31 years and 251 days — most of your working life. Reaching one trillion seconds would take 31,709 years.

For context, 31,709 years ago humans were living in the Stone Age. Woolly mammoths still roamed the earth. The wheel had not been invented. Agriculture did not exist.

That is the difference between a billion and a trillion — not ten times more, not a hundred times more, but roughly 31,000 additional years. Musk does not have a billion dollars. He has 1,230 of them.

How Did He Get Here?

The SpaceX IPO added more than $180 billion to Musk's fortune on IPO day alone. He is now worth more than the next five richest billionaires in the world combined. His personal net worth is larger than the entire GDP of Taiwan, Ireland or Sweden.

It took three years to go from $300 billion to $400 billion. It took approximately four months to go from $800 billion to a trillion. Almost none of that final leg came from Tesla — it came from the repricing of SpaceX, first in private markets and then in the public IPO.

Is It Real Money He Can Spend?

Not exactly. Most of Musk's net worth is unrealised — it exists as the value of shares he owns, not cash in a bank. He cannot simply spend a trillion dollars. To access that money he would need to sell shares, which would itself drive down the price.

The lock-up period after the IPO also means he largely cannot sell his SpaceX shares for 366 days. He can however borrow billions using his shares as collateral, which is how the ultra-wealthy access liquidity without triggering a tax event.

Who Comes Next?

The SpaceX IPO also created approximately 4,400 new SpaceX millionaires among employees and executives who held stock.

As for who might follow Musk into the trillion-dollar club — Larry Page currently sits in second place at approximately $295 billion, followed by Sergey Brin, Jeff Bezos and Larry Ellison, each worth more than $200 billion. None are close to a trillion. Musk got there first. And based on the seconds analogy, the gap between him and second place is not just large — it is geological.
